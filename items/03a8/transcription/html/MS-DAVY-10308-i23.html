HennigBrand

German alchemist who accidentally discovered phosphorus.

✕

Torbern OlafBergman

Swedish chemist and mineralogist. At some point Davy acquired a set of letters from Bergman to Franz Schwediaur written in the 1780s which are retained in the Royal Institution (RI MS HD/27/A). ForMemRSL.

✕

Martin HeinrichKlaproth

Pharmacist and chemist. Professor of Chemistry in Berlin. ForMemRSL.

✕

Peter JacobHjelm

Swedish chemist and the first person to isolate the element molybdenum (1781).

✕

WilliamGregor

Cornish clergyman and mineralogist who discovered the elemental metal titanium. Knew Davy.

✕

Louis NicolasVauquelin

French pharmacist and chemist who met Davy in Paris in 1813. ForMemRSL.

✕

CharlesHatchett

He made his money from coach building which he used to pursue his interests in chemistry and mineralogy in the course of which he isolated the chemical element now called niobium. He was a Manager of the Royal Institution, playing a key role especially in running its lecture programme during the 1800s. FRSL.

✕

Anders GustafEkeberg

Swedish analytical chemist who discovered tantalum in 1802.

✕

SmithsonTennant

Tennant was a botanist and chemist and knew Davy through agricultural improvements. Appointed Professor of Chemistry at Cambridge University in 1813, he was killed in a riding accident on France. FRSL.

✕

William HydeWollaston

Originally a physician he became a prominent chemist, physicist and physiologist isolating the chemical elements osmium, palladium and rhodium. He made his fortune by developing a method of obtaining metallic platinum. A friend and to some extent a rival of Davy's, in 1820 he served briefly as President of the Royal Society of London between Banks's death and Davy's election. FRSL.

✕

Jöns JacobBerzelius

Swedish chemist. He discovered the chemical elements cerium and selenium, and was the first to isolate silicon, thorium, titanium, and zirconium. A rival of Davy's whom he met a few times.

✕

WilhelmHisinger

Swedish physicist and chemist who collaborated with Jöns Jacob Berzelius.

Arsenic

Symbol/formula: As

Arsenic occurs in many minerals, usually in combination with sulphur and metals, but also as a pure elemental crystal. Arsenic is a metalloid.

✕

Colbalt

Symbol/formula: Co

A chemical element, a hard, lustrous, silvery metal.

✕

Manganese

Symbol/formula: Mn

A hard, brittle, silvery metal, often found in minerals in combination with iron. Manganese was first isolated in the 1770s.

✕

Uranium

Symbol/formula: U

Discovered in 1789 by Martin Heinrich Klaproth and named after the newly discovered planet, Uranus.

✕

Tellurium

Symbol/formula: Te

A brittle, mildly toxic, rare, silver-white metalloid. Tellurium is chemically related to selenium and sulphur, all three of which are chalcogens. It is occasionally found in its native form as elemental crystals.

✕

Molybdenum

Symbol/formula: Mo

Chemical element; discovered in 1778 by Carl Wilhelm Scheele.

✕

Chromium

Symbol/formula: Cr

A chemical element; it is a steely-grey, lustrous, hard, and brittle transition metal.

✕

Columbium

Symbol/formula: Nb

Now known as niobium. A light grey, crystalline, and ductile transition metal.

✕

Tantalum

Symbol/formula: Ta

A chemical element previously known as tantalium, it is named after Tantalus, a figure in Greek mythology. Tantalum is a very hard, ductile, lustrous, blue-gray transition metal that is highly corrosion-resistant.

✕

Iridium

Symbol/formula: Ir

A chemical element; a very hard, brittle, silvery-white transition metal of the platinum group.

✕

Osmium

Symbol/formula: Os

A chemical element; a hard, brittle, bluish-white transition metal in the platinum group that is found as a trace element in alloys, mostly in platinum ores.

✕

Palladium

Symbol/formula: Pd

A chemical element; It is a rare and lustrous silvery-white metal discovered in 1802 by the English chemist William Hyde Wollaston

✕

Rhodium

Symbol/formula: Rh

A chemical element; it is a very rare, silvery-white, hard, corrosion-resistant transition metal. It is a noble metal and a member of the platinum group.

✕

Cerium

Symbol/formula: Ce

Cerium is a soft, ductile, and silvery-white metal that tarnishes when exposed to air.
