HenryCavendish

A central figure in English natural philosophy and chemistry, among other research he determined the density of the earth and discovered what would later be named hydrogen. He was one of the people who helped appoint Davy to the Royal Institution. Following his death in 1810 Davy delivered a widely-quoted eulogy on Cavendish and later arranged for some of his apparatus to be transferred to the Royal Institution. FRSL.

✕

Frederick WilliamHerschel

Hanoverian astronomer and composer who while living in Bath discovered the planet later named Uranus. Moving to Slough he built the world's largest telescope to date which Davy visited when he was courting Jane Apreece. FRSL.

✕

John Frederick WilliamHerschel

Son of William Herschel and a ploymathic mathematician, natural philosopher, astronomer and chemist who coined the word photograph. Unsuccesful in being elected President of the Royal Society of London, he later became Master of the Mint. FRSL.

✕

CharlesBlagden

Physician who kept a revealing diary for most of his life. A friend of Joseph Banks serving as a Secretary of the Royal Society of London, 1784-1797; Knighted 1792. FRSL

✕

William HydeWollaston

Originally a physician he became a prominent chemist, physicist and physiologist isolating the chemical elements osmium, palladium and rhodium. He made his fortune by developing a method of obtaining metallic platinum. A friend and to some extent a rival of Davy's, in 1820 he served briefly as President of the Royal Society of London between Banks's death and Davy's election. FRSL.

✕

HenryWarburton

A member of the Animal Chemistry Club, the Chemical Club, and the Geological Society of London, and a sporting friend of Davy's. FRSL.

Hydrogene

Symbol/formula: H

Now known as hydrogen. Hydrogen is the lightest element and, at standard conditions, is a gas of diatomic molecules. It is colourless, odourless, tasteless, non-toxic, and highly combustible.

Carbonic acid

Symbol/formula: CO2 or H2CO3

Now known as Carbon dioxide (CO2), or a solution of carbon dioxide in water (H2CO3).
