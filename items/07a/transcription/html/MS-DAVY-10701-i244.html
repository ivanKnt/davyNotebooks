Carbonate of lime

Symbol/formula: CaCO3

Calcium carbonate.

✕

Lime

Symbol/formula: CaO

Commonly known as quicklime or burnt lime, calcium oxide is a widely used chemical compound. It is a white, caustic, alkaline, crystalline solid at room temperature.

✕

Magnesia

Symbol/formula: MgO

A white hygroscopic solid mineral that occurs naturally as periclase and is a source of magnesium.

✕

Soda

An alkaline substance obtained from the ashes of marine or salt-impregnated plants, especially species of Salsola. Davy decomposed soda to reveal its elemental sodium.

✕

Baryte

Symbol/formula: BaSO4

A mineral consisting of barium sulphate.

✕

Silicious

Symbol/formula: SiO2

Siliceous rocks: any of a group of sedimentary rocks that consist largely or almost entirely of silicon dioxide.

✕

Nitre

Symbol/formula: KNO3

Potassium nitrate. The mineral form is saltpetre, which was useful as a fertiliser and for making gunpowder.

✕

Potassa

Symbol/formula: K2O

Potassium oxide. In his 'Elements of Chemical Philosophy', Pt 1, Vol 1 (1812), Davy describes how potassa is produced from heating potassium at a low temperature, producing a substance containing a large amount of oxygen. When this substance is "heated very strongly", it produces "pure potash or potassa" (pp. 323-324).

William ThomasBrande

Originally trained as an apothecary, Brande became a chemist. With Davy and others founded the Animal Chemistry Club. Lectured on chemistry in various London institutions and, following Davy's retirement succeeded him in his positions at the Royal Institution where he founded The Quarterly Journal of Science. A Secretary of the Royal Society of London, 1816-1826; later fell out with Davy. FRSL.
