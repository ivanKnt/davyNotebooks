Carb amm & chloride of Sulphur gradually brought together - Sulphur
deposited and soluble salts containing Mur & Sul acid Amm no Nit Acid
& on being heated giving much Ammonia with smell of prussic vapour a coloured substance
[also rose] like sulphite of Ammonia & sulphate [remad] at last
The mixed salts in ether gave nothing soluble in alcohol a [solution] [precipitating]
by water ( Sulphur ?) the solution contains muriate & sulphate of amm
when evap & heatd it yields amm & leaves a [caclareous] residue - no
sulphate is taken up by the alcohol that remains & is soluble in wa-
ter Carb acid probably decomposed at same time as amm & cyanogen
formed.
Octr. 13. Bile from Mr. Howship -
Very thick & viscid nearly colourless - little odour - dries up
into a hard horny substance with but little diminution of volume -
dissolves in water giving a solution of Albumen which coagulates by
heat acids & alcohol - Alcohol dissolves nothing out of this bile - no
[bitter] substance or resin - nor is it alkaline Is more like strong white
of egg than any thing else.
- chlorine passed over heated |fuzed| Nitre - a little nitrous gas and
nitrous acid liberated which distilled over - salt took on a yellowish
tint but action not strong - On examination when cold salt
was found to be a mixture of Nitre & chloride Potassium , there
was no chlorate of Potassa formed

Sulphur

Symbol/formula: S

Pure sulphur is a tasteless, odourless, brittle solid that is pale yellow in colour, a poor conductor of electricity, and insoluble in water.

✕

Nitric acid

Symbol/formula: HNO3

A highly corrosive mineral acid. The compound is colourless, but samples tend to acquire a yellow cast over time due to decomposition into oxides of nitrogen

✕

Ammonia

Symbol/formula: NH3

An inorganic chemical compound of nitrogen and hydrogen. Ammonia is a colourless gas with a distinct pungent smell.

✕

Sulphite

Symbol/formula: SO

Any salt or ester of sulphurous acid, containing the ions SO32- or HSO3- (hydrogen sulphite) or the groups -SO3 or -HSO3. The salts are usually soluble crystalline compounds.

✕

Chlorine

Symbol/formula: Cl

Davy named chlorine in 1810 and insisted it was an element and not a compound.

✕

Nitre

Symbol/formula: KNO3

Potassium nitrate. The mineral form is saltpetre, which was useful as a fertiliser and for making gunpowder.

✕

Nitrous oxide

Symbol/formula: N2O

Later known as 'laughing gas'. Davy discovered that nitrous oxide was respirable when not contaminated with nitric oxide (NO). Davy researched into the physiological effects of nitrous oxide.

✕

Potassium

Symbol/formula: K

A silvery white metal that is soft enough to easily cut with a knife. Potassium metal reacts rapidly with atmospheric oxygen to form flaky white potassium peroxide in only seconds of exposure.

✕

Potassa

Symbol/formula: K2O

Potassium oxide. In his 'Elements of Chemical Philosophy', Pt 1, Vol 1 (1812), Davy describes how potassa is produced from heating potassium at a low temperature, producing a substance containing a large amount of oxygen. When this substance is "heated very strongly", it produces "pure potash or potassa" (pp. 323-324).
