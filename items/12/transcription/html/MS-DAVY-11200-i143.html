Iodine

Symbol/formula: I

An element and the heaviest of the group of stable halogens. Bernard Courtois was the first to isolate the substance. Whilst in Paris Davy investigated the substance, and proved it to be a chemical element describing his results in a paper sent to the Royal Society. This caused disputes between Davy and Joseph Louis Gay-Lussac, who had also been researching the element.

✕

Oxygene

Symbol/formula: O

Now known as oxygen. Oxygen is a colourless, odourless, tasteless gas essential to living organisms.

✕

Oxychloric acid

Symbol/formula: HCIO4

Later known as perchloric acid, oxychloric acid was discovered by Friedrich von Stadion. It is stronger than sulfuric, nitric and hydrochloric acids. Von Stadion showed that this acid could be made from chlorine dioxide, which he had also discovered.

✕

Oxypotassame

Davy explains in his paper 'Further Experiments and Observations on Iodine', published in 1814 (Vol 104, see pp. 494-5), that oxyiodes are triple compounds which produce oxygen and iodine when 'decomposed by heat'. Oxypotassame is therefore 'the triple compound of potassium, oxygene, and iodine.'

✕

Potassium

Symbol/formula: K

A silvery white metal that is soft enough to easily cut with a knife. Potassium metal reacts rapidly with atmospheric oxygen to form flaky white potassium peroxide in only seconds of exposure.

✕

Oxybarame

Davy explains in his paper 'Further Experiments and Observations on Iodine', published in 1814 (Vol 104, see pp. 494-5), that oxyiodes are triple compounds which produce oxygen and iodine when 'decomposed by heat'. Oxybarame, therefore, is the triple compound of barium, oxygen and iodine.

✕

Barium

Symbol/formula: Ba

Davy was the first to isolate barium, using electrochemistry, in 1808. It is a soft, silvery alkaline earth metal, which due to its high chemical reactivity is never found in nature as a free element.
