The Royal Society of London

The Royal Society, formally The Royal Society of London for Improving Natural Knowledge, is a learned society and the United Kingdom's national academy of sciences. Founded on 28 November 1660, it was granted a royal charter by King Charles II and is the oldest continuously existing scientific academy in the world.

Argentane

Symbol/formula: AgCl

The Oxford English Dictionary asserts that argentane a word only used by Davy, being his 'name for argentic chloride' (AgCl). The OED's example, from Davy's 'Elements of Chemical Philosophy' published in 1812, shows how Davy writes that 'argentane, has been long known by the name of hornsilver', a mineral composed of silver chloride and also known as ceragyrite.

✕

Muriate of silver

Symbol/formula: AgCl

Silver chloride. A white crystalline solid well-known for its low solubility in water and its sensitivity to light.

✕

Lime

Symbol/formula: CaO

Commonly known as quicklime or burnt lime, calcium oxide is a widely used chemical compound. It is a white, caustic, alkaline, crystalline solid at room temperature.

✕

Silver nitrate

Symbol/formula: AgNO3

Silver is dissolved in nitric acid and this solution is then evaporated to leave a white crystalline solid.

✕

Potash

An alkaline substance consisting largely of impure potassium carbonate. In October 1807, Davy decomposed potash using electricity, revealing the previously unknown element potassium.

✕

Silver oxide

Symbol/formula: Ag2O

A solid compound soluble in alkali solutions and slightly so in water.

✕

Sulphuric acid

Symbol/formula: H2SO4

A mineral acid composed of the elements sulphur, oxygen, and hydrogen. It is a colourless, odourless, and viscous liquid that is miscible with water.

✕

Potassa

Symbol/formula: K2O

Potassium oxide. In his 'Elements of Chemical Philosophy', Pt 1, Vol 1 (1812), Davy describes how potassa is produced from heating potassium at a low temperature, producing a substance containing a large amount of oxygen. When this substance is "heated very strongly", it produces "pure potash or potassa" (pp. 323-324).
