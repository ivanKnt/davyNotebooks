Sunday July . 17
Made a very decisive expt on the
oxybarame in relation to Gay Lussac 's
imagined acid; several grains of oxybarame
were acted on by [x] 3 drops of Sulphuric acid
diluted with water. & boiled for a long
time with it . This oxye . had barely the
power of reddening turmeric & contained very
little barame. - The fluid was filtered off
& the residuum washed till it
did not change litmus . This residuum
heated gave off abundance of Iodine . So
that the oxybarame was in |great| excess
& this was further proved by the solid
matter becoming alkaline like oxybarame .
The fluid evaporated gave a strong acid
substance that reddened vegetable blues
& then destroyed them & that when cooled
was pasty & very deliquescent. This
substance distilled gave Sulphate of Baryta
for a fixed residuum & Sulphuric acid

Oxybarame

Davy explains in his paper 'Further Experiments and Observations on Iodine', published in 1814 (Vol 104, see pp. 494-5), that oxyiodes are triple compounds which produce oxygen and iodine when 'decomposed by heat'. Oxybarame, therefore, is the triple compound of barium, oxygen and iodine.

✕

Sulphuric acid

Symbol/formula: H2SO4

A mineral acid composed of the elements sulphur, oxygen, and hydrogen. It is a colourless, odourless, and viscous liquid that is miscible with water.

✕

Oxygene

Symbol/formula: O

Now known as oxygen. Oxygen is a colourless, odourless, tasteless gas essential to living organisms.

✕

Litmus

A water-soluble mixture of different dyes extracted from lichens used to test materials for acidity.

✕

Iodine

Symbol/formula: I

An element and the heaviest of the group of stable halogens. Bernard Courtois was the first to isolate the substance. Whilst in Paris Davy investigated the substance, and proved it to be a chemical element describing his results in a paper sent to the Royal Society. This caused disputes between Davy and Joseph Louis Gay-Lussac, who had also been researching the element.

✕

Joseph LouisGay-Lussac

French chemist and physicist, known mostly for his discovery that water is made of two parts hydrogen and one part oxygen by volume (with Alexander von Humboldt), for two laws related to gases, and for his work on alcohol-water mixtures. Became involved in controversies with Davy over various chemical discoveries. ForMemRSL.
