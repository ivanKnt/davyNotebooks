✕

Potassame

Symbol/formula: KIO3

Now known as potassium iodate. A compound of potassium and iodine.

✕

Muriate of Potassa

Symbol/formula: KCI

Potassium chloride.

✕

Potassane

A compound of potassium and chlorine.

✕

Nitre

Symbol/formula: KNO3

Potassium nitrate. The mineral form is saltpetre, which was useful as a fertiliser and for making gunpowder.

✕

Iodine

Symbol/formula: I

An element and the heaviest of the group of stable halogens. Bernard Courtois was the first to isolate the substance. Whilst in Paris Davy investigated the substance, and proved it to be a chemical element describing his results in a paper sent to the Royal Society. This caused disputes between Davy and Joseph Louis Gay-Lussac, who had also been researching the element.

✕

Phosphoric acid

Symbol/formula: H3PO4

A corrosive acid that can form three different classes of salts, namely primary phosphates, dibasic phosphates, and tribasic phosphates. It is corrosive to ferrous metals and alloys.

✕

Hydroionic acid

Symbol/formula: HI

Now known as hydroiodic acid. This acid is formed from the combination of iodine with hydrogen. Davy discusses making hydrionic acid in his 'Philosophical Transactions' paper 'Some Experiments and Observations on a New Substance Which Becomes a Violet Coloured Gas by Heat', published in 1814.

✕

Hydrophosphorous acid

In his Royal Society paper 'On Some Combinations of Phosphorus and Sulphur, and on Some Other Subjects of Chemical Inquiry' (Vol 102, 1812), Davy describes his experiments with the fluid compound of phosphorous and oxymuriatic acid, or chlorine. Treated with water, crystals form which Davy calls hydrophosphorous acid, which apparently consists of 'pure phosphorous acid and water' (p. 408).

✕

Potassa

Symbol/formula: K2O

Potassium oxide. In his 'Elements of Chemical Philosophy', Pt 1, Vol 1 (1812), Davy describes how potassa is produced from heating potassium at a low temperature, producing a substance containing a large amount of oxygen. When this substance is "heated very strongly", it produces "pure potash or potassa" (pp. 323-324).

by means of a gold cone

As he described in 'The Bakerian Lecture, on some chemical Agencies of Electricity' (1806), Davy was known to use gold cones as part of his experiments with Voltaic batteries.
