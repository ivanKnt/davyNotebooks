A coastal town in north-western Italy, within the city of Genoa.

Sandstone

A sedimentary rock consisting of sand or quartz grains cemented together, typically red, yellow, or brown in colour.

✕

Slate

Slate is a fine-grained, foliated, homogeneous, metamorphic rock derived from an original shale-type sedimentary rock composed of clay or volcanic ash through low-grade, regional metamorphism

✕

Iodine

Symbol/formula: I

An element and the heaviest of the group of stable halogens. Bernard Courtois was the first to isolate the substance. Whilst in Paris Davy investigated the substance, and proved it to be a chemical element describing his results in a paper sent to the Royal Society. This caused disputes between Davy and Joseph Louis Gay-Lussac, who had also been researching the element.

✕

Baryte

Symbol/formula: BaSO4

In modern nomenclature, a mineral consisting of barium sulphate. In Davy's terminology it might have been equivalent to baryta, BaO.

✕

Lime

Symbol/formula: CaO

Commonly known as quicklime or burnt lime, calcium oxide is a widely used chemical compound. It is a white, caustic, alkaline, crystalline solid at room temperature.

✕

Oxygene

Symbol/formula: O

Now known as oxygen. Oxygen is a colourless, odourless, tasteless gas essential to living organisms.

✕

Hydroionic acid

Symbol/formula: HI

Now known as hydroiodic acid. This acid is formed from the combination of iodine with hydrogen. Davy discusses making hydrionic acid in his 'Philosophical Transactions' paper 'Some Experiments and Observations on a New Substance Which Becomes a Violet Coloured Gas by Heat', published in 1814.

✕

Nitrous oxide

Symbol/formula: N2O

Later known as 'laughing gas'. Davy discovered that nitrous oxide was respirable when not contaminated with nitric oxide (NO). Davy researched into the physiological effects of nitrous oxide.

✕

Silver nitrate

Symbol/formula: AgNO3

Silver is dissolved in nitric acid and this solution is then evaporated to leave a white crystalline solid.

✕

Chlorionic acid

In his paper 'Some Experiments on a Solid Compound of Iodine and Oxygene, and on Its Chemical Agencies', published for the Royal Society in 1815, Davy described chlorionic acid as 'the substance produced by the agency of
the combination of iodine and chlorine in water' (Vol 105, 1815).

✕

Nitric acid

Symbol/formula: HNO3

A highly corrosive mineral acid. The compound is colourless, but samples tend to acquire a yellow cast over time due to decomposition into oxides of nitrogen
