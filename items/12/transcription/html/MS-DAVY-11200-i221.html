Nitre

Symbol/formula: KNO3

Potassium nitrate. The mineral form is saltpetre, which was useful as a fertiliser and for making gunpowder.

✕

Charcoal

A lightweight black carbon residue produced by strongly heating wood (or other animal and plant materials) in minimal oxygen to remove all water and volatile constituents.

✕

Nitrous oxide

Symbol/formula: N2O

Later known as 'laughing gas'. Davy discovered that nitrous oxide was respirable when not contaminated with nitric oxide (NO). Davy researched into the physiological effects of nitrous oxide.

✕

Oxygene

Symbol/formula: O

Now known as oxygen. Oxygen is a colourless, odourless, tasteless gas essential to living organisms.

✕

Potassa

Symbol/formula: K2O

Potassium oxide. In his 'Elements of Chemical Philosophy', Pt 1, Vol 1 (1812), Davy describes how potassa is produced from heating potassium at a low temperature, producing a substance containing a large amount of oxygen. When this substance is "heated very strongly", it produces "pure potash or potassa" (pp. 323-324).

✕

Nitric acid

Symbol/formula: HNO3

A highly corrosive mineral acid. The compound is colourless, but samples tend to acquire a yellow cast over time due to decomposition into oxides of nitrogen

✕

Nitrogene

Symbol/formula: N

Now known as nitrogen. A colourless and odourless gas, nitrogen is non-combustible and non-toxic. Davy also refers to this as azote.

✕

Mercury

Symbol/formula: Hg

Also known as quicksilver. A heavy, silvery element, mercury is the only metallic element that is known to be liquid at standard temperature and pressure.

✕

Carbonic acid

Symbol/formula: CO2 or H2CO3

Now known as Carbon dioxide (CO2), or a solution of carbon dioxide in water (H2CO3).

✕

Muriatic acid

Symbol/formula: HCl

Hydrochloric acid. Davy also refers to this as muriatic gas and marine acid.

✕

Daniel GabrielFahrenheit

German physicist, inventor, and scientific instrument maker, who invented the mercury-in-glass thermometer. The temperature scale he proposed is named after him. ForMemRSL.
