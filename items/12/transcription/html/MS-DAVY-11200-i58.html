Antoine-LaurentLavoisier

French chemist and tax farmer (for which he was guillotined). Developed a new nomenclature for chemistry (much of which is still in use today) and argued strongly against the existence of phlogiston. Davy read some of his writings while in Cornwall which he then opposed and throughout his career maintained an ambivalent attitude towards Lavoisierian chemisty. ForMemRSL.

✕

SmithsonTennant

Tennant was a botanist and chemist and knew Davy through agricultural improvements. Appointed Professor of Chemistry at Cambridge University in 1813, he was killed in a riding accident in France. FRSL.

✕

WilliamAllen

Allen was a Quaker who worked in the pharmaceutical industry. Davy asked him to give lectures on natural philosophy at the RI. FRSL.

✕

William HaseldinePepys

Pepys was a surgical and scientific instrument maker and chemist. Constructed large batteries used by Davy (amongst others). FRSL.

✕

Jean-BaptisteBiot

French mathematician, physicist and astronomer; Professor of Physics at the Collège de France. ForMemRSL.

✕

Dominique François JeanArago

French mathematician, physicist, astronomer and political liberal. Davy presented him with the Copley Medal in 1825. ForMemRSL.

✕

Louis Bernard Guyton de Morveau

French chemist and revolutionary politician who is credited with producing the first systematic method of chemical nomenclature. ForMemRSL.

✕

Diamond

a precious stone consisting of a clear and colourless crystalline form of pure carbon, the hardest naturally occurring substance. Davy was the first to prove that diamond was pure carbon in Italy: 'Some Experiments on the Combustion of the Diamond and Other Carbonaceous Substances', Philosophical Transactions of the Royal Society of London, Vol. 104 (1814), pp. 557-570.

✕

Oxygene

Symbol/formula: O

Now known as oxygen. Oxygen is a colourless, odourless, tasteless gas essential to living organisms.

✕

Hydrogene

Symbol/formula: H

Now known as hydrogen. Hydrogen is the lightest element and, at standard conditions, is a gas of diatomic molecules. It is colourless, odourless, tasteless, non-toxic, and highly combustible.

✕

Bakerian Lecture

The Bakerian Lecture was founded by Henry Baker FRS (1698 - 1774) in 1774 with a bequest. It is considered the most prestigious lecture in natural history and experimental philosophy.
