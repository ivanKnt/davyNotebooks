Sulphuric acid

Symbol/formula: H2SO4

A mineral acid composed of the elements sulphur, oxygen, and hydrogen. It is a colourless, odourless, and viscous liquid that is miscible with water.

✕

Potassa

Symbol/formula: K2O

Potassium oxide. In his 'Elements of Chemical Philosophy', Pt 1, Vol 1 (1812), Davy describes how potassa is produced from heating potassium at a low temperature, producing a substance containing a large amount of oxygen. When this substance is "heated very strongly", it produces "pure potash or potassa" (pp. 323-324).

Lime

Symbol/formula: CaO

Commonly known as quicklime or burnt lime, calcium oxide is a widely used chemical compound. It is a white, caustic, alkaline, crystalline solid at room temperature.

Muriate of lime

Symbol/formula: CaCl2

An inorganic compound and a white crystalline solid at room temperature. Calcium chloride is highly soluble in water, and can be created by neutralising hydrochloric acid with calcium hydroxide.

Chlorine

Symbol/formula: Cl

Hydrogene

Symbol/formula: H

Now known as hydrogen. Hydrogen is the lightest element and, at standard conditions, is a gas of diatomic molecules. It is colourless, odourless, tasteless, non-toxic, and highly combustible.

AlessandroVolta

Professor of experimental physics at the University of Pavia. At the end of the eighteenth century he Invented the Voltaic pile which produced electric current and which Davy shortly dubbed the battery. Davy described meeting him in 1814 in notebook 14/I, p.38. ForMemRSL.

William HydeWollaston

Originally a physician he became a prominent chemist, physicist and physiologist isolating the chemical elements osmium, palladium and rhodium. He made his fortune by developing a method of obtaining metallic platinum. A friend and to some extent a rival of Davy's, in 1820 he served briefly as President of the Royal Society of London between Banks's death and Davy's election. FRSL.
