Table of the Combination of Light

Draft of a table which appeared in Th. Beddoes 'Contributions to Physical and Medical Knowledge' Bristol 1799, page 91

✕

Oxygene

Symbol/formula: O

Now known as oxygen. Oxygen is a colourless, odourless, tasteless gas essential to living organisms.

✕

Phosoxygen

Davy's working name for the combination of light and oxygen. He published his theories in a collection of essays entitled On Heat, Light, and the Combinations of Light, On Phos-oxygen and its Combinations (1799).

✕

Nitrogene

Symbol/formula: N

Now known as nitrogen. A colourless and odourless gas, nitrogen is non-combustible and non-toxic. Davy also refers to this as azote.

✕

Muriatic acid

Symbol/formula: HCl

Hydrochloric acid. Davy also refers to this as muriatic gas and marine acid.

✕

Mercury

Symbol/formula: Hg

Also known as quicksilver. A heavy, silvery element, mercury is the only metallic element that is known to be liquid at standard temperature and pressure.

✕

Lead

Symbol/formula: Pb

A heavy metal that is denser than most common materials. Lead is soft and malleable, and also has a relatively low melting point.

✕

Tin

Symbol/formula: Sn

A silvery-coloured metal, tin is soft enough to be cut with little force, and a bar of tin can be bent by hand with little effort.

✕

Iron

Symbol/formula: Fe

A strong, hard, magnetic silvery-grey metal. It is, by mass, the most common element on Earth, just ahead of oxygen, forming much of Earth's outer and inner core.

✕

Zinc

Symbol/formula: Zn

A a slightly brittle metal at room temperature and has a shiny-greyish appearance when oxidation is removed.

✕

Manganese

Symbol/formula: Mn

A hard, brittle, silvery metal, often found in minerals in combination with iron. Manganese was first isolated in the 1770s.

✕

Wolfram

Symbol/formula: W

Also known as tungsten. Tungsten is an exceptionally strong refractory metal. It was identified as a new element in 1781 and first isolated as a metal in 1783.
