Philos. A mere change of colour is
no proof of the nature of a chemical
change; It would not follow if Carbon
were liberated from the blood that it
must necessarily become brighter Sulphur
in combining with iron | charcoal | becomes a clear
fluid; & a black oxide of copper
becomes red in writing with a substance
which abounds in Carbon , - prussic gas
or Cyanosene It was the loose analogy
of the change of colour of the protoxide
of iron by the action of oxygen which
led to the rude chemical idea of the
colour of the blood depending upon iron
& its oxygenation. No change in sensible
qualities can ever indicate with precision
the nature of a chemical change,

Carbon

Symbol/formula: C

A non-metallic and tetravalent element. Well-known allotropes include graphite, diamond, amorphous carbon, and fullerenes.

✕

Sulphur

Symbol/formula: S

Pure sulphur is a tasteless, odourless, brittle solid that is pale yellow in colour, a poor conductor of electricity, and insoluble in water.

✕

Charcoal

A lightweight black carbon residue produced by strongly heating wood (or other animal and plant materials) in minimal oxygen to remove all water and volatile constituents.

✕

Copper oxide

Symbol/formula: CuO

Copper II oxide is an inorganic compound formed of the elements copper and oxygen. It can be used in applications such as pottery to produce red, green or blue colourings.

✕

Prussic acid

Symbol/formula: HCN

Hydrogen cyanide. A colourless, extremely poisonous, and flammable liquid that boils slightly above room temperature.

✕

Oxygene

Symbol/formula: O

Now known as oxygen. Oxygen is a colourless, odourless, tasteless gas essential to living organisms.
