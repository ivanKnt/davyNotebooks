To the fire flies, the
same place & summer
written in blank verse but not Rhyme
in May & June 1827.
Ye moving stars that flit along
the glade
Ye animated lamps that midst the shade
Of ancient chesnuts on the lofty hills
Of Lucignano , by the foaming rills
[xxxx]
Which feed Blue Serchio , in the evening
play.
Making the twilight your
So bright your light that in the unbroken
ray
Of the meridian moon it brightly shines
How lightly gaily do ye pass beneath
the vines
Which clothe our nearest slopes
Now through the groves
Of Lucca do ye dance, the breeze that moves
Their silver leaves, a mountain Zephyrs
[xxxxx]
wing

Ye moving stars that flit along

John Davy published this poem in his Memoirs; see Collected Works, vol. 1, pp. 251-2

✕

meridian moon

The meridian is the north-south line in the sky. When the moon is crossing the meridian it is in the South and at the highest point of its daily arc.

✕

Zephyr

Greek mythology: god of the west wind. His ame is sometimes used to describe a breeze that is gentle or westerly.
