HenryCavendish

A central figure in English natural philosophy and chemistry, among other research he determined the density of the earth and discovered what would later be named hydrogen. He was one of the people who helped appoint Davy to the Royal Institution. Following his death in 1810 Davy delivered a widely-quoted eulogy on Cavendish and later arranged for some of his apparatus to be transferred to the Royal Institution. FRSL.

✕

CharlesBlagden

Physician who kept a revealing diary for most of his life. A friend of Joseph Banks serving as a Secretary of the Royal Society of London, 1784-1797; Knighted 1792. FRSL

✕

BenjaminThompson

Born in Massachusetts, he served in the British and Bavarian armies. Knighted by George III in 1787, the Elector of Bavaria created him Reichsgraf von Rumford of the Holy Roman Empire in 1792. His appointment in the late 1790s as Bavarian ambassador to London created a minor diplomatic incident, during which he helped establish and manage the Royal Institution, in the course of which he appointed Davy to lecture there. Later he married Lavoisier's widow and lived unhappily ever after. FRSL.

✕

JosephBanks

A member of the Lincolnshire gentry, Banks sailed as a botanist on James Cook's first voyage to the Pacific (1768-1771). Elected President of the Royal Society of London in 1778 he served for forty-two years until just before his death. The dominant figure in the running of English science during that time, he became one of Davy's patrons and ultimately Davy succeeded him to the Presidency. FRSL.

✕

NevilMaskelyne

Astronomer Royal and a Commissioner of the Board of Longitude. Contributed to scientifically measuring the density of the Earth. FRSL.

✕

ThomasBeddoes

A politically radical (Jacobin) chemist and physician who in the 1790s established the Medical Pneumatic Institution in Bristol to investigate the physiological and possible curative effects of all the gases discovered during the eighteenth century. Between late 1798 and early 1801 he employed Davy as Superintendent of the Institution.

✕

JamesWatt

A Scottish scientific instrument maker who studied with the chemist Joseph Black at Glasgow University. He developed the separate condenser for steam engines which increased their effieciency significantly. He took out a twenty-five-year patent (ending in 1800) and moved to Birmingham where he went into partnership with the businessman and industrialist Matthew Boulton. They made their fortune by having an effective monopoly of manufacturing steam engines. A friend of Thomas Beddoes, he met Davy on a number of occasions and Davy gave the oration at the unveiling of Watt's statue in Greenock. FRSL.

✕

MattthewBoulton

Birmingham businessman and industrialist who went into partnership with James Watt to manufacture efficient steam engines using Watt's patent for the separate condenser. They made their fortune by having an effective monopoly of manufacturing steam engines until the patent expired in 1800. An early Proprietor of the Royal Institution, he had grave doubts about Rumford's proposal to freely display inventions there. FRSL.

✕

FrancisHome

Physician and Professor of Materia Medica at the University of Edinburgh, 1768-1798.

✕

CharlesHatchett

He made his money from coach building which he used to pursue his interests in chemistry and mineralogy in the course of which he isolated the chemical element now called niobium. He was a Manager of the Royal Institution, playing a key role especially in running its lecture programme during the 1800s. FRSL.

✕

Edward CharlesHoward

A brother of the Duke of Norfolk, he became a chemist, discovering the explosive properties of mercury fulminate. FRSL

✕

SmithsonTennant

Tennant was a botanist and chemist and knew Davy through agricultural improvements. Appointed Professor of Chemistry at Cambridge University in 1813, he was killed in a riding accident on France. FRSL.

✕

William HydeWollaston

Originally a physician he became a prominent chemist, physicist and physiologist isolating the chemical elements osmium, palladium and rhodium. He made his fortune by developing a method of obtaining metallic platinum. A friend and to some extent a rival of Davy's, in 1820 he served briefly as President of the Royal Society of London between Banks's death and Davy's election. FRSL.

Michel EugèneChevreul

French organic chemist. Met Davy when he was in Paris in 1813. ForMemRSL.

RichardKirwan

Irish chemist, mineralogist and geologist who accused fellow geologist, James Hutton, of athiesm following publication of his Theory of the Earth (1788). FRSL

Louis NicolasVauquelin

French pharmacist and chemist who met Davy in Paris in 1813. ForMemRSL.

✕

Pierre LouisDulong

French physicist and chemist who worked mostly on specific heats, but also discovered nitrogen chloride around the same time as Davy. ForMemRSL.

✕

WilliamAllen

Allen was a Quaker who worked in the pharmaceutical industry. Davy asked him to give lectures on natural philosophy at the RI. FRSL.

✕

William HaseldinePepys

Pepys was a surgical and scientific instrument maker and chemist. Constructed large batteries used by Davy (amongst others). FRSL.

✕

DaviesGiddy

Changed his surname to Gilbert in 1817. A member of the West Cornwall gentry, he was one of Davy's earliest patrons. A former student of Beddoes at Oxford University, he was a key figure in securing Davy's appointment in 1798 to the Medical Pneumatic Institution. He maintained contact with Davy throughout his life and following his death suceeded Davy as President of the Royal Society of London. FRSL.

Louis Bernard Guyton de Morveau

French chemist and revolutionary politician who is credited with producing the first systematic method of chemical nomenclature. ForMemRSL.

✕

Gaspard deProny

French mathematician and engineer. ForMemRSL.

✕

André-MarieAmpère

French physicist and mathematician and one of the key figure in the early study of electro-magnetism. Davy met him in Paris in 1813 and they developed a scientific rivalry. ForMemRSL.

✕

TeodoroMonticelli

Neapolitan naturalist with an interest in volcanology and the mineralogy of Vesuvius. One of Davy's closest contacts in Naples.

✕

GeorgesCuvier

French naturalist and zoologist who was one of the founders of palaeontology. Perpetual Secretary of the first class of the Institut de France (Académie des Sciences), Davy met him when in Paris in 1813. ForMemRSL.

✕

WilliamBuckland

Anglican priest who was later Dean of Westminster. Perhaps the last serious geologist to attempt the reconciliation of the findings of contemporary geology with the Biblical accounts. FRSL.

✕

GeorgeKnox

Irish politician who sat in the Dublin and then Westminster parliaments. A collector of minerals, it is not known how he came to know Davy. FRSL.

✕

JohnPlayfair

Professor of Natural Philosophy at Edinburgh University. His Illustrations of the Huttonian Theory of the Earth (1802) gave strong support to James Hutton's theory of uniformitarianism. FRSL.

✕

Thomas CharlesHope

Professor of Medicine and Chemistry at Edinburgh University. Discovered the element strontium and seems to have played a role in Davy's appointment to the Royal Institution. FRSL.

✕

JamesHall

Scottish baronet (1776) and MP. Chemist and geologist who supported of James Hutton's theories of uniformitarian geology. FRSL.

✕

BryanHiggins

Irish chemist and natural philosopher who developed a form of the atomic theory of matter. Worked in London, St Petersburg and Jamacia. Uncle of William Higgins.

✕

WilliamHiggins

Nephew of Bryan Higgins. Professor of Chemistry at the Dublin Society where he proposed a theory of chemical atomism. Met Davy in London who supported Higgins's claim to the atomic theory over that of John Dalton. FRSL.

✕

HeinrichOlbers

Astronomer who worked in Bremen. He specialised in the detection of asteroids and propounded his eponymous paradox. Davy met him on a visit to Bremen in 1824. ForMemRSL.

✕

Carl FriedrichGauss

Prominent Hanoverian mathematician who was Director of the observatory at Gottingen University. Davy met him during a visit to Bremen in 1824. ForMemRSL.

✕

Heinrich ChristianSchumacher

Founder and Directort of the Altona astronomical observatory. Davy met him during a visit to Bremen in 1824. ForMemRSL.

✕

George IV

Eldest son of George III and Prince of Wales. Served as Prince Regent during the King's madness, 1811-1820, thereafter King of England, Scotland and Ireland. A major patron of the arts, he personally commanded Davy to go to Naples to oversee the unrolling of the Herculaneum papyri. PatronRSL.

Caroline

Of Brunswick-Wolfenbüttel who in 1795 married the Prince of Wales (later Prince Regent and George IV. They soon separated causing a series of scandals that lasted until her death. The Davys tended to take her side.

From 1798 6th Lord Byron. Famous poet who knew both Humphry and Jane Davy in London and Italy; he wrote a slightly risqué skit on their marriage in 1812. FRSL.

RobertSouthey

Poet who met Davy in Bristol and included some of his poems in Annual Anthology. Poet Laureate from 1813 until death.

Prominent portrait painter and President of the Royal Academy, 1820-1830. Painted the famous portrait of Davy as President of the Royal Society of London. FRSL.

Prominent English sculptor who, at the request of Lady Davy, made the memorial plaque to Davy placed in Westminster Abbey. FRSL.

WilliamWordsworth

English poet who, with Samuel Taylor Coleridge, published Lyrical Ballads. Davy contributed to seeing the second edition (1800) through the press in Bristol. Poet Laureate from 1843.
