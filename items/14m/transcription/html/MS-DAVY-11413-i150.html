You are well acquainted with
the vague notions which prevailed
before the middle of the last
century respecting the shock of the
Torpedo I will not dwell upon the
ideas of Redi or Borelli that the effect
was a mechanical one owing to
peculiar motions or vibrations of
the organs which possess the power
of giving the shock. Walsh you know
was considered as having proved the
phenomenon to be purely electrical
& Mr Cavendish compared the organ
of the Torpedo to a large surface
very weakly charged & considered
the effects as depending upon elec-
-tricity in very large quantity but of
very low intensity. Volta when he
made his wonderful discovery of
the pile or Galvanic column
imagined he had formed a perfect
artificial resemblance of the
organ of the Torpedo & his views
have been generally adopted.

JohnWalsh

Nabob and later an MP he experimented on the electrical properties of torpedo fish. FRSL.

✕

HenryCavendish

A central figure in English natural philosophy and chemistry, among other research he determined the density of the earth and discovered what would later be named hydrogen. He was one of the people who helped appoint Davy to the Royal Institution. Following his death in 1810 Davy delivered a widely-quoted eulogy on Cavendish and later arranged for some of his apparatus to be transferred to the Royal Institution. FRSL.

AlessandroVolta

Professor of experimental physics at the University of Pavia. At the end of the eighteenth century he Invented the Voltaic pile which produced electric current and which Davy shortly dubbed the battery. Davy described meeting him in 1814 in notebook 14/I, p.38. ForMemRSL.

LuigiGalvani

Italian surgeon and anatomist who carried out experiments with electricity and animals. The term 'galvanism' was named after him
