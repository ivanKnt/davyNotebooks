Muriate of tin

Symbol/formula: SnCl4

Stannic chloride. Tin chloride is a white crystalline solid. It forms a stable dihydrate, but aqueous solutions tend to undergo hydrolysis, particularly if hot.

✕

Iron

Symbol/formula: Fe

A strong, hard, magnetic silvery-grey metal. It is, by mass, the most common element on Earth, just ahead of oxygen, forming much of Earth's outer and inner core.

✕

Prussic acid

Symbol/formula: HCN

Hydrogen cyanide. A colourless, extremely poisonous, and flammable liquid that boils slightly above room temperature.

✕

Lime

Symbol/formula: CaO

Commonly known as quicklime or burnt lime, calcium oxide is a widely used chemical compound. It is a white, caustic, alkaline, crystalline solid at room temperature.

✕

Muriatic acid

Symbol/formula: HCl

Hydrochloric acid. Davy also refers to this as muriatic gas and marine acid.

✕

Oxygene

Symbol/formula: O

Now known as oxygen. Oxygen is a colourless, odourless, tasteless gas essential to living organisms.

✕

Phosphorus

Symbol/formula: P

A non-metallic chemical element of the nitrogen family. A colourless, semitransparent, soft, waxy solid.

✕

Hydrogene

Symbol/formula: H

Now known as hydrogen. Hydrogen is the lightest element and, at standard conditions, is a gas of diatomic molecules. It is colourless, odourless, tasteless, non-toxic, and highly combustible.

✕

Copper

Symbol/formula: Cu

A soft, malleable, and ductile metal with very high thermal and electrical conductivity. A freshly exposed surface of pure copper has a pinkish-orange colour.

✕

Manganese

Symbol/formula: Mn

A hard, brittle, silvery metal, often found in minerals in combination with iron. Manganese was first isolated in the 1770s.

✕

Sulphur

Symbol/formula: S

Pure sulphur is a tasteless, odourless, brittle solid that is pale yellow in colour, a poor conductor of electricity, and insoluble in water.

✕

Nitrous oxide

Symbol/formula: N2O

Later known as 'laughing gas'. Davy discovered that nitrous oxide was respirable when not contaminated with nitric oxide (NO). Davy researched into the physiological effects of nitrous oxide.

✕

Phosoxygen

Davy's working name for the combination of light and oxygen. He published his theories in a collection of essays entitled On Heat, Light, and the Combinations of Light, On Phos-oxygen and its Combinations (1799).

✕

Antoine-Francois deFourcroy

French chemist who collaborated with Lavoisier in standardising chemical nomenclature. Davy as a teenager read some of his work.

✕

Claude LouisBerthollet

French Chemist. ForMemRSL.

✕

Louis Bernard Guyton de Morveau

French chemist and revolutionary politician who is credited with producing the first systematic method of chemical nomenclature. ForMemRSL.

✕

JanIngenhousz

Dutch-born British physiologist, biologist and chemist. He was one of the discoverers of the process of photosynthesis. FRSL.

✕

ArmandSeguin

French chemist. He took over the editorial duties of the Annals de Chimie after Lavoisier's execution in 1794
