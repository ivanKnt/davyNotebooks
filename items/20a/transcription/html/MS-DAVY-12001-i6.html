Louis Bernard Guyton de Morveau

French chemist and revolutionary politician who is credited with producing the first systematic method of chemical nomenclature. ForMemRSL.

✕

Antoine-LaurentLavoisier

French chemist and tax farmer (for which he was guillotined). Developed a new nomenclature for chemistry (much of which is still in use today) and argued strongly against the existence of phlogiston. Davy read some of his writings while in Cornwall which he then opposed and throughout his career maintained an ambivalent attitude towards Lavoisierian chemisty. ForMemRSL.

✕

Phosphate of lime

Symbol/formula: Ca3(PO4)2

Tricalcium phosphate (sometimes abbreviated TCP) is a calcium salt of phosphoric acid.

✕

Manganese

Symbol/formula: Mn

A hard, brittle, silvery metal, often found in minerals in combination with iron. Manganese was first isolated in the 1770s.

✕

Sulphuric acid

Symbol/formula: H2SO3

A mineral acid composed of the elements sulphur, oxygen, and hydrogen. It is a colourless, odourless, and viscous liquid that is miscible with water.

✕

Muriatic acid

Symbol/formula: HCl

Hydrochloric acid. Davy also refers to this as muriatic gas and marine acid.

✕

Nitrous acid

Symbol/formula: HNO2

A weak and monoprotic acid known only in solution, in the gas phase and in the form of nitrite salts.

✕

Oxide of mercury

Symbol/formula: HgO

An inorganic compound, mercury oxide is red or orange in colour, and a solid at room temperature and pressure. The mineral form montroydite is very rarely found.

✕

Ammonia

Symbol/formula: NH3

An inorganic chemical compound of nitrogen and hydrogen. Ammonia is a colourless gas with a distinct pungent smell.

✕

Phosoxygen

Davy's working name for the combination of light and oxygen. He published his theories in a collection of essays entitled On Heat, Light, and the Combinations of Light, On Phos-oxygen and its Combinations (1799).

✕

Platina

An alloy of platinum and several other metals, including palladium, osmium, and iridium.

✕

Caloric

A hypothetical fluid substance formerly thought to be responsible for the phenomena of heat.
