9.
- Observations on Galvanism -
In perusing Mr Nicholsons account of
his curious experiments & those of Mr Carlisle
on the decomposition of water by means of
the Galvanic battery of Volta I was particularly
struck with the phenomenon of the fixation
of the Oxygene at the point of one wire
whilst & the |extraction of the| Hydrogene was given out at
the round the surface of the other the
intermediate distance being nearly two
inches. In Whilst pursuing a
course of expts with a view of collecting
facts which might lead to an explanation
of this phaenomenon I have met with made
some new observations
In pursuing a course of experiments on the
Galvanic influence cheifly with a view to
collect facts towards an explanation of
a curious phaenomenon noticed by Mr Nicholsons
in the account of his curious expts & those
of Mr Carlisle on the decomposition of
water by [an] namely the oxydation of the
wire fixation of Oxygene & at the point
of one wire & the extrication of Hydrogene
from the other, the intermediate distance
betw between them being considerable I have
been a met with some new phaenomena
as some of them lead to a new
application of this extraordinary power

Reference

Humphry Davy (1800)Account of some Experiments made with the Galvanic Apparatus of Signor Volta.. A Journal of Natural Philosophy, Chemistry, and the Arts, 275-81.

✕

WilliamNicholson

Founding editor of A Journal of Natural Philosophy, Chemistry, and the Arts (1797-1814) where Davy published some of his early papers. Part of the Godwin circle.

✕

AnthonyCarlisle

London surgeon and anatomist. FRSL.

✕

LuigiGalvani

Italian surgeon and anatomist who carried out experiments with electricity and animals. The term 'galvanism' was named after him to refer to the generation of electric current by chemical action.

✕

AlessandroVolta

Professor of experimental physics at the University of Pavia. At the end of the eighteenth century he Invented the Voltaic pile which produced electric current and which Davy shortly dubbed the battery. Davy described meeting him in 1814 in notebook 14/I, p.38. ForMemRSL.

✕

Oxygene

Symbol/formula: O

Now known as oxygen. Oxygen is a colourless, odourless, tasteless gas essential to living organisms.

✕

Hydrogene

Symbol/formula: H

Now known as hydrogen. Hydrogen is the lightest element and, at standard conditions, is a gas of diatomic molecules. It is colourless, odourless, tasteless, non-toxic, and highly combustible.
